üìù Key Concepts

Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)

  • Presentation: Continuous murmur at left 2nd intercostal space, radiates to clavicles, loud S2, bounding pulses
  • Pathophysiology: Ductus arteriosus fails to close after birth ‚Üí left-to-right shunt from aorta to pulmonary artery
  • Complications: Eisenmenger syndrome (pulmonary hypertension with shunt reversal), heart failure if uncorrected
  • Treatment: Indomethacin or ibuprofen (prostaglandin inhibitors) to close ductus; surgical ligation if medical therapy fails

Mitral Stenosis

  • Presentation: Opening snap followed by diastolic rumble at apex, loud S1
  • Cause: Usually rheumatic heart disease, chronic fibrotic/calcific changes
  • Hemodynamics: Increased left atrial pressure ‚Üí pulmonary edema, atrial fibrillation
  • Treatment: Medical optimization, surgical valve replacement if severe

Aortic Stenosis

  • Presentation: Crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur at upper right sternal border, radiates to carotids
  • Associations: Bicuspid aortic valve, age-related calcific changes
  • Classic triad: Angina, syncope, heart failure (SAD = Syncope, Angina, Dyspnea)

Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)

  • Presentation: Fixed split S2 + low-grade ejection murmur
  • Most common type: Ostium secundum defect (associated with Trisomy 21 for primum type)
  • Pathophysiology: Left-to-right shunt ‚Üí RV volume overload ‚Üí right heart failure

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Untreated PDA with chronic left-to-right shunting ‚Üí pulmonary hypertension ‚Üí Eisenmenger syndrome (shunt reversal, right-to-left)
  • Think PDA in premature infants; indomethacin closes it by blocking prostaglandins
  • Mitral stenosis ‚Üí atrial fibrillation ‚Üí stroke risk (anticoagulate!)
  • Fixed split S2 = ASD until proven otherwise

Practice Question 1

A 4-month-old infant presents with failure to thrive and tachypnea. On exam, you hear a continuous murmur at the left upper sternal border that does not change with respiration. The infant has widened pulse pressure. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • Ventricular septal defect
  • Patent ductus arteriosus
  • Tetralogy of Fallot
  • Coarctation of the aorta
Answer: B - Patent ductus arteriosus

Explanation: The continuous "machinery" murmur that persists throughout systole and diastole, combined with widened pulse pressure (bounding pulses) is classic for PDA. The murmur is best heard at the left 2nd intercostal space. VSD causes a holosystolic murmur, not continuous.

Practice Question 2

A 42-year-old woman with a history of rheumatic fever presents with dyspnea on exertion. Cardiac auscultation reveals an opening snap followed by a low-pitched diastolic rumble heard best at the apex. What valve is affected?

  • Aortic valve
  • Mitral valve
  • Tricuspid valve
  • Pulmonic valve
Answer: B - Mitral valve

Explanation: The opening snap followed by diastolic rumble at the apex is pathognomonic for mitral stenosis. Rheumatic heart disease is the most common cause. The opening snap occurs when the stenotic mitral valve opens.

üìù Key Concepts

Airway Management in Trauma

  • Expanding neck hematoma: Immediate life-threatening emergency ‚Üí endotracheal intubation FIRST before any other intervention
  • Cricothyrotomy/tracheostomy: Reserved for failed intubation, not first-line for expanding hematoma
  • Rule: Airway > Breathing > Circulation (ABCs)

Cervical Spine Trauma

  • Clinical clearance: NEXUS criteria or Canadian C-spine rules determine if imaging is needed
  • Imaging of choice: CT cervical spine (superior sensitivity to plain x-ray)
  • MRI: For suspected ligamentous injury, spinal cord injury, or neurologic deficits
  • Key point: All trauma patients get c-spine immobilization until cleared clinically or by imaging

Arterial Injury (Extremity)

  • Hard signs: Pulsatile bleeding, expanding hematoma, absent pulses, bruit/thrill, ischemia
  • Management: Immediate surgical exploration + repair (no time for imaging if hard signs present)
  • Femoral artery penetrating trauma: Surgical exploration is the answer when absent pulses or hard signs

Chest Trauma

  • Pulmonary contusion: Blunt chest trauma ‚Üí alveolar/interstitial edema ‚Üí respiratory distress, supportive care
  • Ruptured bronchus: Persistent air leak, subcutaneous emphysema, pneumothorax ‚Üí requires surgical repair
  • Tension pneumothorax: Hemodynamic compromise, tracheal deviation ‚Üí needle decompression THEN chest tube

Blunt Renal Trauma

  • Imaging of choice: CT scan of abdomen and kidneys with IV contrast
  • Indications: Hemodynamically stable patient with gross hematuria or microscopic hematuria + mechanism
  • Management: Most managed conservatively; surgery for expanding hematoma or vascular injury

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Expanding neck hematoma = INTUBATE NOW (airway compromise imminent)
  • Hard signs of arterial injury = IMMEDIATE surgical exploration (no CT angiography)
  • CT c-spine is imaging of choice in trauma; use NEXUS/Canadian rules to decide if imaging needed
  • Ruptured bronchus: Think of it when persistent air leak despite chest tube
  • Renal trauma: CT with contrast is key; most can be managed non-operatively

Practice Question 1

A 16-year-old girl is brought to the ED after being stabbed in the anterior neck. A large hematoma is evident and pulsatile. As you examine her, the hematoma expands. What is the most appropriate initial step?

  • Barium esophagography
  • Endotracheal intubation
  • Esophagoscopy
  • Indirect laryngoscopy
  • Tracheostomy
Answer: B - Endotracheal intubation

Explanation: Airway obstruction is an immediate life-threatening emergency. The expanding hematoma can compress the trachea causing airway compromise. Secure the airway FIRST with endotracheal intubation before any other diagnostic or therapeutic intervention. Tracheostomy would be considered if intubation fails.

Practice Question 2

A 27-year-old woman is brought to the ED after a motor vehicle collision. She has abdominal and left flank pain. She is hemodynamically stable. Urinalysis shows gross blood. What is the most appropriate next step?

  • Renal blood flow scan
  • CT scan of abdomen and kidneys
  • Arteriography
  • Peritoneal lavage
  • Exploratory operation
Answer: B - CT scan of abdomen and kidneys

Explanation: CT scan with IV contrast is the imaging modality of choice for blunt renal trauma in hemodynamically stable patients. It can assess the extent of injury (contusion, laceration, hematoma, vascular injury) and guide management. Most blunt renal injuries are managed conservatively.

üìù Key Concepts

Upper GI Bleeding (Gastritis)

  • Presentation: Coffee-ground emesis, melena, hematemesis, tachycardia, dry mucous membranes
  • Initial management: IV fluid resuscitation (2 large-bore IVs, crystalloids) - ALWAYS before endoscopy
  • Definitive diagnosis/treatment: Upper endoscopy (EGD) after stabilization
  • Rule: ABG and NG tube do NOT replace volume resuscitation as first priority

Ulcerative Colitis

  • Presentation: Bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain, tenesmus, weight loss
  • Complications: Toxic megacolon (dilated colon >6cm, fever, abdominal tenderness)
  • Pathophysiology: Chronic inflammation limited to colon, starts in rectum, continuous
  • Imaging: Barium enema shows "lead pipe" colon, loss of haustra
  • Treatment: Medical (5-ASA, corticosteroids) ‚Üí surgery for refractory/toxic megacolon

Crohn Disease

  • Presentation: Abdominal pain, diarrhea (often bloody), weight loss, RLQ tenderness
  • Key features: Skip lesions, transmural inflammation, terminal ileum involvement
  • Imaging: "String sign" on barium study (narrowed terminal ileum), cobblestoning
  • Complications: Fistulas, strictures, abscesses, malabsorption

Bacterial Overgrowth (Post-Billroth II)

  • Cause: Loss of normal transit after gastric bypass ‚Üí bacterial overgrowth in blind loop
  • Presentation: Diarrhea, bloating, weight loss, vitamin deficiencies (B12), steatorrhea
  • Treatment: Antibiotics to reduce bacterial burden

Intussusception (Pediatric)

  • Buzzwords: "Currant jelly" stools, colicky pain, drawing up knees, palpable mass RUQ
  • Imaging: "Target sign" on ultrasound (telescoped bowel)
  • Treatment: Contrast enema (both diagnostic and therapeutic); surgery if reduction fails
  • Age group: Infants and young children (6-36 months)

Peptic Ulcer Disease (Duodenal)

  • Cause: H. pylori infection, NSAIDs, stress
  • Pathophysiology: Scarring and fibrosis ‚Üí gastric outlet obstruction (chronic)
  • Presentation: Epigastric pain, bloating, succussion splash, vomiting of undigested food
  • Treatment: Triple/quadruple therapy for H. pylori; endoscopic balloon dilation or surgery for stricture

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Upper GI bleed: IV fluids FIRST, then endoscopy - never skip resuscitation
  • Ulcerative colitis ‚Üí toxic megacolon risk; watch for fever, dilated colon, peritoneal signs
  • Crohn: Skip lesions, fistulas, "cobblestone" appearance - can affect anywhere mouth to anus
  • Intussusception: Contrast enema reduces AND diagnoses (kills two birds with one stone)
  • Post-Billroth II: Think bacterial overgrowth if diarrhea + malabsorption

Practice Question 1

A 26-year-old woman with chronic alcoholism presents with hematemesis. She is tachycardic (124/min), hypotensive (92/68 mm Hg), and has dry blood-caked oral mucous membranes. What is the most appropriate first step?

  • Measurement of arterial blood gases
  • Intravenous administration of fluids
  • Intravenous infusion of ADH (vasopressin)
  • Insertion of nasogastric tube
  • Upper gastroduodenal endoscopy
Answer: B - Intravenous administration of fluids

Explanation: This patient has signs of hypovolemic shock from GI bleeding (tachycardia, hypotension, dry mucous membranes). The FIRST priority is IV volume resuscitation with crystalloids to restore hemodynamic stability. After stabilization, upper endoscopy can be performed for diagnosis and treatment.

Practice Question 2

An 11-month-old girl presents with fever, passing purple jelly-like stools, and episodes of drawing up her knees while crying. An elongated mass is palpated in the right upper abdominal quadrant. What is the most appropriate next step?

  • Contrast enema
  • Upper GI series with small bowel follow-through
  • CT scan of abdomen
  • HIDA scan
  • Colonoscopy
Answer: A - Contrast enema

Explanation: This is classic intussusception (currant jelly stools, colicky pain, palpable mass). Contrast enema is both diagnostic (shows "target sign") and therapeutic (reduces the intussusception via hydrostatic pressure). Ultrasound can also diagnose it, but contrast enema is preferred as it's therapeutic.

Low TSH + high T3/T4 Toxic Adenoma: Autonomous thyroid hormone production ‚Üí negative feedback suppresses TSH. "Hot" nodule on radioiodine scan. Treat with thyroidectomy. üìù Key Concepts

Toxic Adenoma (Hyperthyroidism)

  • Presentation: Heat intolerance, sweating, weight loss, palpitations, tremor, diarrhea
  • Labs: Low TSH (negative feedback), elevated T3 and T4
  • Imaging: Radioiodine uptake scan shows "hot" solitary nodule
  • Treatment: Partial thyroidectomy (surgical removal of adenoma)

Adrenal Crisis (Addisonian Crisis)

  • Trigger: Physiologic stress (surgery, infection, trauma) in patient with adrenal insufficiency
  • Presentation: Hypotension refractory to fluids, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia
  • Emergency treatment: Administer hydrocortisone IV (both mineralocorticoid and glucocorticoid effects)
  • Key point: Don't wait for labs - give hydrocortisone immediately if suspected

Insulinoma

  • Classic triad: 1) Hypoglycemia symptoms, 2) Low glucose <45 mg/dL, 3) Relief with glucose (Whipple's triad)
  • Labs: High insulin, high C-peptide, low glucose
  • Imaging: CT or MRI of pancreas, endoscopic ultrasound
  • Treatment: Surgical resection of tumor

Chronic Pancreatitis

  • Cause: Recurrent acute pancreatitis ‚Üí destruction of acinar and islet cells
  • Consequences: Exocrine insufficiency (malabsorption) + endocrine insufficiency (diabetes)
  • Treatment: Pancreatic enzyme replacement + insulin therapy for diabetes
  • Key to reducing morbidity: Insulin therapy prevents hyperglycemia complications

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Toxic adenoma: Autonomous T3/T4 production ‚Üí negative feedback suppresses TSH
  • Adrenal crisis in surgical patient: Give hydrocortisone BEFORE surgery if history of steroid use
  • Insulinoma: Fasting hypoglycemia that improves with eating; C-peptide high (endogenous insulin)
  • Chronic pancreatitis ‚Üí diabetes: Requires insulin replacement (not sulfonylureas)

Practice Question 1

A 52-year-old woman comes to the physician with a palpable left thyroid nodule. Labs show TSH is decreased and T3 and T4 are increased. Ultrasound shows a solid 1-cm mass. Radioiodine scan shows increased uptake in the nodule. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • Graves disease
  • Hashimoto thyroiditis
  • Toxic adenoma
  • Papillary thyroid carcinoma
  • Medullary thyroid carcinoma
Answer: C - Toxic adenoma

Explanation: A "hot" nodule (increased uptake on radioiodine scan) with suppressed TSH and elevated thyroid hormones is diagnostic of a toxic adenoma. The adenoma autonomously produces T3/T4, suppressing TSH via negative feedback. Treatment is partial thyroidectomy.

Practice Question 2

A 32-year-old woman with SLE on long-term prednisone presents for laparoscopic cholecystectomy. On induction of propofol, her BP drops to 60/40 despite fluid administration. What is the most appropriate next step?

  • Administer diphenhydramine
  • Administer dopamine
  • Administer gentamicin
  • Administer hydrocortisone
  • Decrease dose of propofol
Answer: D - Administer hydrocortisone

Explanation: This is an adrenal crisis (Addisonian crisis). Chronic steroid use suppresses the HPA axis, and during stress (surgery), the adrenal glands cannot produce sufficient cortisol. The hypotension is refractory to fluids. Immediate IV hydrocortisone is lifesaving. Stress-dose steroids should be given perioperatively to patients on chronic steroids.

üîë Key Buzzwords - Click to Learn

Claudication Leg pain with exertion, improves with rest. Atherosclerosis ‚Üí arterial stenosis. BEST initial treatment: Walking program (improves collateral circulation). Virchow triad DVT risk factors: (1) Stasis, (2) Endothelial injury, (3) Hypercoagulability. Catheter = endothelial injury. Diagnose with duplex ultrasound. Amaurosis fugax Transient monocular vision loss ("curtain coming down"). Retinal artery embolism from carotid stenosis. Needs carotid endarterectomy if symptomatic.

üìù Key Concepts

Peripheral Vascular Disease (Claudication)

  • Presentation: Leg pain with exertion, improves with rest (classic claudication)
  • Pathophysiology: Atherosclerosis ‚Üí arterial stenosis ‚Üí inadequate blood flow during exercise
  • First-line treatment: Walking program (exercise improves collateral circulation)
  • Risk factor modification: Stop smoking, control hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia
  • Medical: Aspirin, cilostazol (phosphodiesterase inhibitor)
  • Surgical: Bypass, angioplasty/stenting for severe disease or rest pain

Deep Venous Thrombosis (DVT)

  • Virchow triad: Stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability
  • Catheter-associated DVT: Infrapopliteal vein thrombosis from central line/chemotherapy
  • Diagnosis: Duplex ultrasound (compression ultrasonography)
  • Treatment: Anticoagulation (heparin ‚Üí warfarin or DOAC)
  • Complications: Pulmonary embolism, post-thrombotic syndrome

Carotid Stenosis

  • Amaurosis fugax: Transient monocular vision loss (retinal artery embolism)
  • Pathophysiology: Atherosclerotic plaque ‚Üí embolization or reduced flow
  • Diagnosis: Carotid duplex ultrasound or CT/MR angiography
  • Treatment: Carotid endarterectomy for symptomatic >50% stenosis or asymptomatic >70%

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Claudication: Exercise is the BEST initial treatment (improves collateral flow)
  • DVT risk factors: Immobility, surgery, cancer, oral contraceptives, hypercoagulable states
  • Amaurosis fugax = "curtain coming down" ‚Üí think carotid stenosis
  • Catheter-related DVT: Remove catheter if infected; anticoagulate
  • ABI <0.9 = PAD; <0.4 = critical limb ischemia (rest pain, tissue loss)

Practice Question 1

A 57-year-old man with a 40-year smoking history presents with calf pain that occurs after walking half a mile and resolves with 5 minutes of rest. Ankle-brachial index is 0.6. What is the most appropriate initial treatment?

  • Immediate surgical bypass
  • Recommend a walking program
  • CT scan of lumbar spine
  • MRI of the calf
  • Aspirin therapy
Answer: B - Recommend a walking program

Explanation: This is classic intermittent claudication from peripheral arterial disease. The BEST initial treatment is a structured walking/exercise program, which improves collateral circulation. Risk factor modification (smoking cessation, aspirin) is also important. Surgery is reserved for severe disease or lifestyle-limiting symptoms refractory to conservative measures.

Practice Question 2

A 68-year-old man experiences a 5-minute episode of complete vision loss in his right eye, described as a "curtain coming down." The episode resolved spontaneously. Carotid duplex shows 80% stenosis of the right internal carotid artery. What is the most appropriate treatment?

  • Aspirin and observation
  • Carotid endarterectomy
  • Anticoagulation with warfarin
  • MRI of the brain
Answer: B - Carotid endarterectomy

Explanation: Amaurosis fugax (transient monocular vision loss) is a TIA from retinal artery embolism, indicating symptomatic carotid stenosis. Carotid endarterectomy is indicated for symptomatic stenosis >50% (NASCET criteria). This patient has 80% stenosis with symptoms, making CEA the definitive treatment.

üîë Key Buzzwords - Click to Learn

Conjugated hyperbilirubinemia Biliary Atresia: Direct bilirubin >2 mg/dL or >20% total in newborn. Progressive jaundice, pale stools. Kasai procedure before 2-3 months for best outcome. Kasai procedure Hepatoportoenterostomy for biliary atresia. Connects liver to small bowel to restore bile flow. Must be done early. May still need liver transplant later. üìù Key Concepts

Biliary Atresia

  • Presentation: Progressive jaundice, pale stools, dark urine, poor weight gain in newborn (weeks 2-8 of life)
  • Labs: Conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinemia >50% of total
  • Pathophysiology: Progressive fibrosis and obliteration of extrahepatic bile ducts
  • Diagnosis: HIDA scan, cholangiography, or exploratory surgery with liver biopsy
  • Treatment: Kasai procedure (hepatoportoenterostomy) - restores bile flow
  • Prognosis: May require liver transplant if Kasai fails

Intussusception

  • Age: 6-36 months (peak 6-12 months)
  • Presentation: Colicky abdominal pain (legs drawn up), "currant jelly" stools, palpable RUQ mass
  • Pathophysiology: Telescoping of bowel (usually ileum into cecum) ‚Üí obstruction
  • Diagnosis: Ultrasound shows "target sign" or "donut sign"
  • Treatment: Contrast enema (air or water-soluble) - diagnostic AND therapeutic
  • Surgery: If enema reduction fails or perforation occurs

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Biliary atresia: Direct bilirubin >2 mg/dL or >20% of total = conjugated hyperbilirubinemia
  • Kasai procedure must be done early (before 2-3 months) for best outcomes
  • Intussusception: Contrast enema kills two birds - diagnoses AND treats
  • Pathologic lead point (Meckel, polyp, lymphoma): Consider in children >3 years

Practice Question 1

A 3-week-old newborn presents with progressive jaundice, scleral icterus, and generalized jaundice. Labs show total bilirubin 15 mg/dL with direct component 13 mg/dL. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • ABO incompatibility
  • Biliary atresia
  • Breast milk jaundice
  • Gilbert syndrome
  • Physiologic jaundice
Answer: B - Biliary atresia

Explanation: Conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinemia with progressively worsening jaundice in the first weeks of life is classic for biliary atresia. Direct bilirubin >2 mg/dL is always pathologic. Diagnosis requires HIDA scan, cholangiography, or liver biopsy. Treatment is the Kasai procedure (hepatoportoenterostomy).

ü©∏ Hematology & Transfusion

üîë Key Buzzwords - Click to Learn

FNHTR Febrile Non-Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction: Fever/chills 1-6hrs post-transfusion. Antibodies to donor WBC antigens. Prevent with leukoreduction. Dilutional thrombocytopenia Massive transfusion (>10 units PRBC) ‚Üí platelet dilution ‚Üí oozing. Give platelets to maintain >50,000/mm¬≥ for surgery. üìù Key Concepts

Febrile Non-Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction (FNHTR)

  • Presentation: Fever, chills within 1-6 hours of transfusion (no hemolysis)
  • Cause: Preformed antibodies to donor leukocyte antigens or cytokines in plasma
  • Treatment: Stop transfusion, rule out hemolysis (check labs), antipyretics
  • Prevention: Leukoreduction of transfused blood products

Thrombocytopenia (Dilutional)

  • Cause: Massive transfusion (>10 units PRBC) ‚Üí dilution of platelets
  • Presentation: Oozing from operative sites, petechiae, mucosal bleeding
  • Treatment: Platelet transfusion to maintain >50,000/mm¬≥ for surgery

Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

  • Pathophysiology: Antiplatelet antibodies ‚Üí splenic destruction
  • Presentation: Petechiae, purpura, mucosal bleeding, normal bone marrow
  • Treatment: Steroids, IVIG; splenectomy for refractory cases

Antithrombin III Deficiency

  • Presentation: Recurrent DVTs, early-onset thrombosis, family history
  • Lab: AT3 activity <50-70% (qualitative or quantitative deficiency)
  • Treatment: Lifelong anticoagulation; may require AT3 concentrate

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • FNHTR: Most common transfusion reaction; benign but rule out hemolysis
  • Thrombocytopenia + oozing post-transfusion: Give platelets
  • ITP refractory to steroids/IVIG ‚Üí splenectomy (removes site of platelet destruction)
  • AT3 deficiency: Unprovoked DVT in young patient ‚Üí check hypercoagulable workup

Practice Question 1

A 45-year-old man develops fever and chills 2 hours after receiving a packed RBC transfusion. His vital signs are stable. There is no flank pain, dark urine, or hemoglobinuria. Labs show no evidence of hemolysis. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • Acute hemolytic transfusion reaction
  • Febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction
  • Transfusion-related acute lung injury
  • Anaphylactic reaction
Answer: B - Febrile non-hemolytic transfusion reaction

Explanation: FNHTR is the most common transfusion reaction. It presents with fever and chills without hemolysis (no dark urine, no elevated LDH/indirect bilirubin, no decreased haptoglobin). It is caused by antibodies to donor leukocyte antigens. Treatment is antipyretics. Prevention is leukoreduction of blood products.

üîë Key Buzzwords - Click to Learn

Non-caseating granulomas Sarcoidosis: Key histologic finding. Vs TB which has caseating granulomas. Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy + lung infiltrates. Treat with steroids. FEV1 <800 mL High risk for postop pulmonary complications after pneumonectomy. Mortality >12%. FEV1 is BEST predictor of postoperative pulmonary function.

üìù Key Concepts

Sarcoidosis

  • Presentation: Shortness of breath, cough (asymptomatic common)
  • Imaging: Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy with/without interstitial infiltrates
  • Histology: Non-caseating granulomas (key finding)
  • Diagnosis: Clinical + imaging + biopsy (lymph node or lung)
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids for symptomatic disease

Preoperative Pulmonary Risk Assessment

  • FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second): Best predictor of postop pulmonary function
  • High risk: FEV1 <800 mL (or predicted FEV1 <40% after pneumonectomy)
  • Mortality rate: >12% if FEV1 <800 mL
  • DLCO: Also useful for assessing postoperative risk

Breast Cancer

  • Screening: Mammography (age 40-50 depending on guidelines); ultrasound for dense breasts
  • Workup: Palpable mass ‚Üí mammogram + ultrasound ‚Üí core needle biopsy (NOT FNA alone for solid masses)
  • Staging: TNM system; sentinel lymph node biopsy for clinically node-negative patients
  • Treatment: Lumpectomy + radiation (equivalent survival to mastectomy for early stage); mastectomy for large/multicentric
  • Receptor status: ER/PR+ ‚Üí tamoxifen/aromatase inhibitor; HER2+ ‚Üí trastuzumab; Triple negative ‚Üí chemotherapy

Colon Cancer

  • Screening: Colonoscopy starting age 45, every 10 years; earlier if family history or Lynch/FAP
  • Presentation: Right-sided = iron deficiency anemia, occult blood; Left-sided = obstruction, change in stool caliber
  • Staging: CT chest/abdomen/pelvis + CEA level; CEA used for monitoring recurrence (not screening)
  • Treatment: Surgical resection with adequate margins; adjuvant chemo for stage III (node positive)
  • Lynch syndrome (HNPCC): Autosomal dominant, mismatch repair gene mutations, right-sided colon cancer
  • FAP: APC gene mutation, hundreds of polyps ‚Üí prophylactic colectomy by age 25

Lung Cancer

  • Solitary pulmonary nodule: Compare to old imaging; if new or growing ‚Üí CT-guided biopsy or PET scan

  • Anatomy: C8 contributes to ulnar nerve (primarily C8 + T1)

Compartment Syndrome

  • Cause: Fracture (especially tibial shaft), crush injury, tight cast, reperfusion after ischemia

  • Ranson criteria / APACHE II: Prognostic scoring (not diagnostic)

Femoral hernia Below inguinal ligament through femoral canal. More common in women. HIGHEST risk of incarceration/strangulation. Always repair surgically (even if asymptomatic). Incarcerated hernia Hernia contents cannot be reduced. If blood supply compromised = strangulated (surgical emergency!). Signs: tenderness, erythema, fever, SBO symptoms.

üìù Key Concepts

Inguinal Hernias

  • Indirect: Lateral to inferior epigastric vessels, through internal ring; congenital (patent processus vaginalis)
  • Direct: Medial to inferior epigastric vessels, through Hesselbach triangle; acquired, older patients
  • Hesselbach triangle: Inguinal ligament (inferior), rectus sheath (medial), inferior epigastric vessels (lateral)
  • Treatment: Surgical repair (mesh repair standard); open or laparoscopic

Femoral Hernia

  • Location: Below inguinal ligament, medial to femoral vein
  • Demographics: More common in women (but inguinal hernias are still most common in women overall)
  • Key point: Highest risk of incarceration/strangulation of all hernias ‚Üí always repair

Incarcerated vs. Strangulated

  • Incarcerated: Cannot be reduced manually; may cause SBO
  • Strangulated: Compromised blood supply ‚Üí ischemia/necrosis; surgical EMERGENCY
  • Signs of strangulation: Severe tenderness, erythema, fever, peritoneal signs, leukocytosis
  • Treatment: Emergent surgery; do NOT attempt reduction if strangulation suspected

Other Hernias

  • Umbilical: Common in infants, most close by age 5; repair if >2cm or persists past age 5
  • Incisional: Through prior surgical incision; risk factors: obesity, infection, poor nutrition
  • Richter hernia: Only part of bowel wall (antimesenteric border) herniates ‚Üí can strangulate without SBO

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Indirect = LATERAL to inferior epigastric vessels; Direct = MEDIAL (mnemonic: MDs are DIRECT)
  • Femoral hernia = highest strangulation risk ‚Üí always repair even if asymptomatic
  • Strangulated hernia: Do NOT attempt manual reduction ‚Üí emergent surgery
  • Richter hernia: Partial bowel wall involvement ‚Üí can strangulate without obstruction signs

üìù Key Concepts

Phases of Wound Healing

  • Hemostasis (minutes): Platelet plug, fibrin clot formation
  • Inflammatory (days 1-4): Neutrophils (first 24-48 hrs) ‚Üí macrophages (most important cell for wound healing)
  • Proliferative (days 4-21): Fibroblasts deposit collagen (type III first ‚Üí replaced by type I), angiogenesis, granulation tissue
  • Remodeling (weeks to months): Type III collagen replaced by type I, wound contraction; max tensile strength ~80% at 8 weeks

Factors Impairing Wound Healing

  • Local: Infection (#1 cause of impaired healing), ischemia, radiation, foreign bodies
  • Systemic: Malnutrition (albumin <3), diabetes, steroids, chemotherapy, smoking, vitamin C deficiency
  • Vitamin C deficiency: Impaired collagen cross-linking (hydroxylation of proline/lysine) ‚Üí poor wound healing, scurvy
  • Zinc deficiency: Impaired cell division and immune function ‚Üí poor healing

Wound Closure Types

  • Primary intention: Clean wound closed immediately (surgical incision)
  • Secondary intention: Wound left open to heal by granulation (contaminated/infected wounds)
  • Tertiary (delayed primary): Wound left open initially, then closed after 3-5 days (contaminated wound, cleaned then closed)

Burns Management

  • Superficial (1st degree): Painful, red, no blisters (sunburn). Heals in 3-5 days
  • Partial thickness (2nd degree): Blisters, painful, moist. Superficial partial ‚Üí heals in 2-3 weeks; deep partial ‚Üí may need grafting
  • Full thickness (3rd degree): Painless, leathery, waxy. Requires excision and skin grafting
  • Fluid resuscitation: Parkland formula: 4 mL √ó kg √ó %TBSA (¬Ω in first 8 hrs from time of burn)
  • Escharotomy: Circumferential full-thickness burns ‚Üí compartment syndrome risk ‚Üí escharotomy to release pressure
  • Inhalation injury: Singed nasal hairs, hoarse voice, carbonaceous sputum ‚Üí early intubation

Wound Dehiscence & Evisceration

  • Risk factors: Malnutrition, obesity, diabetes, steroids, wound infection, coughing/straining
  • Warning sign: Serosanguinous ("salmon-colored") drainage from wound on days 5-8
  • Evisceration: Bowel protrudes through wound ‚Üí cover with moist saline gauze ‚Üí emergent OR

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Macrophages are the MOST important cell in wound healing (direct fibroblasts, angiogenesis)
  • Infection is the #1 cause of impaired wound healing
  • Maximum wound tensile strength is only ~80% of original (never reaches 100%)
  • Parkland formula: Calculate from time of BURN, not time of arrival to hospital
  • Circumferential burn ‚Üí escharotomy (not fasciotomy) to prevent compartment syndrome
  • Salmon-colored drainage from wound = impending dehiscence ‚Üí inspect wound and prepare for OR
  • Full thickness burns are painless (nerve endings destroyed)

A 35-year-old man is rescued from a house fire with burns to his entire anterior trunk and both arms. He weighs 80 kg. Using the Parkland formula, how much fluid should he receive in the first 8 hours?

  • 11,520 mL
  • 2,880 mL
  • 7,680 mL
Answer: A - 5,760 mL

Explanation: TBSA: Anterior trunk (18%) + both arms (9% √ó 2 = 18%) = 36%. Parkland formula: 4 mL √ó 80 kg √ó 36% = 11,520 mL total in 24 hours. Half is given in the first 8 hours: 11,520 √∑ 2 = 5,760 mL. Use Lactated Ringer's solution.
